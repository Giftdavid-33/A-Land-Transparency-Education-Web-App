Before You Pay

Land Buying Checklist

Complete every step before handing over any money. No exceptions.

📜
Step 01
Confirm the Land Title
Verify the document type — C of O, Gazette, or Excision — and confirm it is valid and government-issued.
🏛
Step 02
Search the Land Registry
Visit the State Land Registry to confirm there are no encumbrances, disputes, or government acquisitions on the land.
🗺
Step 03
Verify the Survey Plan
Cross-check the survey plan coordinates with SURCON (Surveyors Council of Nigeria) to confirm the plan is legitimate.
👤
Step 04
Confirm Rightful Ownership
Request valid ID from the seller. Cross-reference with title documents. Be wary of multiple sellers for a single plot.
🔎
Step 05
Physical Inspection
Visit the land in person. Confirm boundaries match the survey plan. Talk to neighbours about any disputes.
⚖️
Step 06
Engage a Lawyer
All transactions should be concluded by a registered legal practitioner. Never sign without legal review.
Know Your Documents

Document Verification Guide

Understanding these documents can save you from losing millions of naira to fraudsters.

Ownership Proof

Certificate of Occupancy (C of O)

The gold standard of Nigerian land documents. Issued by the State Governor under the Land Use Act, it confirms the holder's legal right to occupy the land for 99 years.

Government Record

Gazette

An official government publication recording excised land. It confirms the land has been officially recognised and released by the government. Always verify the date and volume number.

Release Approval

Excision

A government-approved release of land from government acquisition, typically for community or family land. Excised land is removed from the gazette and returned to original owners.

Technical Drawing

Survey Plan

A technical document prepared by a licensed surveyor showing exact land coordinates, dimensions, and boundaries. Must be stamped and signed by a registered surveyor. Verify with SURCON.

Family Land

Deed of Assignment

A legal document transferring ownership from one party to another. It must be executed by a lawyer, stamped by the Land Registry, and registered. Beware of unregistered deeds.

⚠️ Caution

Red Flag Documents

Be wary of photocopies without originals, documents with altered dates, informal receipts as primary proof, and any seller refusing a Land Registry search. Serious warning signs.
